# Stable Quadrature Schemes

## Composite Quadratures

When (one thinks that) $f$ is not very smooth, a better approximation to $\mathcal{I}(f)$ may be obtained by subdividing $[a,b]$ into $m$ subintervals $\{[x_{i-1},x_i]\}_{i=1}^m$, each of equal length $h:=(b-a)/m$ and apply an interpolatory quadrature formula of small degree $n$ in each subinterval $[x_{i-1},x_i]$.

For example, using the trapezium rule on $[x_{i-1},x_i]$, we find

$$
\begin{align}
   \mathcal{C}_{1,m}(f) &= \frac{b-a}m\, \Bigl[ \Bigl( \frac{1}{2} f(x_0) + \frac{1}{2} f(x_1) \Bigr)
	+ \Bigl(\frac{1}{2} f(x_1) + \frac{1}{2} f(x_2) \Bigr) + \cdots
	+ \Bigl( \frac{1}{2} f(x_{m-1}) + \frac{1}{2} f(x_m) \Bigr) \Bigr] \notag\\
   &= h\, \Bigl[ \frac{1}{2} f(x_0) + f(x_1) + f(x_2) + \cdots
	+ f(x_{m-1}) + \frac{1}{2} f(x_m) \Bigr].
\end{align}
$$

This scheme involves $m+1$ evaluations of $f$. To use Simpson's rule in $[x_{i-1},x_i]$, we need to introduce the extra
node $x_{i-1/2}:=(x_{i-1}+x_i)/2$. The resulting scheme is

$$
\begin{align}
   \mathcal{C}_{2,m}(f) &= \frac{b-a}{m} \, \Bigl[ \frac{1}{6} f(x_0) + \frac{2}{3} f(x_{1/2}) + \frac{1}{6} f(x_1)
	+ \frac{1}{6} f(x_1) + \frac{2}{3} f(x_{3/2}) + \frac{1}{6} f(x_2)\\
	& \phantom{= \frac{b-a}{m} \, \Bigl[} + \cdots + \frac{1}{6} f(x_{m-1}) + \frac{2}{3} f(x_{m-1/2}) + \frac{1}{6} f(x_m)\Bigr]\\
	&= h\, \Bigl[  \frac{1}{6} f(x_0) + \frac{2}{3} f(x_{1/2}) + \frac{1}{3} f(x_1) + \frac{2}{3} f(x_{3/2})\\
	&\phantom{= \frac{b-a}{m} \, \Bigl[}  + \cdots + \frac{1}{3} f(x_{m-1}) + \frac{2}{3} f(x_{m-1/2}) + \frac{1}{6} f(x_m) \Bigr],
\end{align}
$$

It involves $2m+1$ evaluations of $f$.

Error bounds for these methods can be obtained directly from those of the underlying methods. For $\mathcal{C}_{1,m}$ we have, in the subinterval $[x_{i-1},x_i]$,

$$
\bigl|\mathcal{I}(f)-\mathcal{I}_1(f)\bigr| \le \frac{1}{2!}\,\max_{\xi\in[x_{i-1},x_i]}\,|f''(\xi)| \,\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_i} |\omega_2(x)| dx = \frac{h^3}{12}\,\max_{\xi\in[x_{i-1},x_i]}\,|f''(\xi)|,
$$

where $\omega_2(x)=(x-x_{i-1})(x-x_i)$. Adding these up, we have in $[a,b]=[x_0,x_m]$,

$$
\bigl|\mathcal{I}(f)-\mathcal{C}_{1,m}(f)\bigr|	\le \frac{mh^3}{12}\,\max_i\Bigl\{\max_{\xi\in[x_{i-1},x_i]}\,|f''(\xi)|\Bigr\} = (b-a)\,\frac{h^2}{12}\,\max_{\xi\in[a,b]}\,|f''(\xi)|.
$$

## Gauss Quadratures

It is clear that the degree of exactness of an interpolatory quadrature $\mathcal{Q}_n(f)$ is at least $n$.
By considering $f(x)=(x-x_0)^2\cdots(x-x_n)^2$, we find that the degree of exactness of any interpolatory quadrature is
no more than $2n+1$. Gauss showed that by judicious choice of the nodes $\{x_k\}$, one could indeed obtain a quadrature exact for all $f \in \mathcal{P}_{2n+1}$. The resulting Gauss quadrature rests on the following

```{admonition} Fact: Gauss quadrature
:class: prf:theorem
The interpolatory formula

$$
\mathcal{G}_n(f) := (b-a) \sum_{k=0}^n \sigma_k f(x_k)
$$

has degree of exactness $2n+1$ if and only if $\{x_k\}$ is chosen such that $\hat p(x) := (x-x_0) \cdots (x-x_n)$ satisfies

$$
\int_a^b \hat p(x) \, p(x) \, dx = 0 \qquad \forall \, p \in \mathcal{P}_n.
$$
```

Thus, the nodes $\{x_k\}_{k=0}^n$ are the roots of a polynomial $\phi_{n+1} \in \mathcal{P}_{n+1}$ which is in the orthogonal complement of $\mathcal{P}_n$ in $\mathcal{P}_{n+1}$ with respect to the inner product $\langle p, q \rangle = \int_a^b p(x) \, q(x) \, dx$. 

Once the nodes are determined, the coefficients can be computed as usual. Unlike the coefficients $\sigma_k$ of a general quadrature formula,  Gauss quadrature weights are always positive, as can be seen by considering $\bar p_k(x)=\hat p(x)^2/(x-x_k)^2 \in \mathcal{P}_{2n}$: Due to the degree of exactness $2n+1$ and $\bar p_k(x_j) = \delta_{jk}$,

$$
0 < \mathcal{I}(p_k) = \mathcal{Q}(p_k) = \sigma_k.
$$

## Self-check questions

```{admonition} **Question**
:class: tip
Show

$$
\bigl|\mathcal{I}(f)-\mathcal{C}_{2,m}(f)\bigr| \le (b-a)\,\frac{h^3}{192}\,\max_{\xi\in[a,b]}\,|f'''(\xi)|.
$$
```

````{dropdown} **Answer**
Using

$$
\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_i} |\omega_3(x)| dx = h^4 \int_0^1 \bigl|(t-0)(t-1/2)(t-1)\bigr| dt = \frac{h^4}{32},
$$

we find

$$
\bigl|\mathcal{I}(f)-\mathcal{C}_{2,m}(f)\bigr| \le \frac{m}{3!}\,\frac{h^4}{32}\,\max_{\xi\in[a,b]}\,|f'''(\xi)| = (b-a)\,\frac{h^3}{192}\,\max_{\xi\in[a,b]}\,|f'''(\xi)|.
$$

```{note}
When $f$ is smooth enough, we could actually prove with a more sophisticated argument taking the symmetry of the nodes into account that

$$
\bigl|\mathcal{I}(f)-\mathcal{C}_{2,m}(f)\bigr| = (b-a)\,\frac{h^4}{2880}\,\max_{\xi\in[a,b]}\,|f^{(4)}(\xi)|.
$$
```
````
